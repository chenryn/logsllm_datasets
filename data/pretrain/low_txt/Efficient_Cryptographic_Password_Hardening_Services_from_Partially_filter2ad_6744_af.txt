### Pythia and Non-Interactive Problem P = (I, V)

Given a non-interactive problem \( P = (I, V) \), the meta-reduction \( M \) operates as follows:

1. **Initialization:**
   - Upon receiving an input problem instance \( x \), the meta-reduction \( M \) forwards \( x \) to the reduction \( R \) to obtain a public key \( pk \) generated by \( R \).
   - \( M \) then randomly selects:
     - A tweak \( t \) uniformly from \(\{0, 1\}^*\),
     - Two messages \( m_0, m_1 \) uniformly from \(\{0, 1\}^*\),
     - Two random values \( r_0, r_1 \) uniformly from \(\{0, 1\}^*\).

2. **First Protocol Run:**
   - \( M \) emulates an honest user instance \( C(t, m_0) \) using randomness \( r_0 \) by sending \( (t, H_2(m_0)^{r_0}) \) to \( R \).
   - \( M \) saves the result \( y_0 \) output by \( R \).

3. **Reset and Second Protocol Run:**
   - \( M \) resets \( R \) to the state where \( R \) had just output the public key \( pk \).
   - \( M \) again emulates \( C(t, m_1) \) using randomness \( r_1 \) by sending \( (t, H_2(m_1)^{r_1}) \) to \( R \).
   - \( M \) receives the result \( y_1 \) from this run.

4. **Output to Reduction:**
   - The meta-reduction \( M \) sends \( (t, m_0, y_1 / r_0) \) and \( (t, m_1, y_1 / r_1) \) to the reduction \( R \).
   - When \( R \) outputs a tentative problem solution \( y' \), \( M \) also outputs \( y' \).

### Analysis of Distributions

Assuming the reduction always allows the adversary to compute a valid output on the first run, the pairs \((m_0, z_0)\) and \((m_1, z_1)\) output by the magic adversary and the meta-reduction are identically distributed. This can be seen as follows:

- **Independence of Messages:**
  - \( m_0 \) and \( m_1 \) are chosen independently in both cases, so their respective results \( z_0 \) and \( z_1 \) are also independent.
  - We can consider the distributions of \((m_0, z_0)\) and \((m_1, z_1)\) separately.

- **Transcript Independence:**
  - The transcripts resulting from \( m_0 \) and \( m_1 \) are independent of the messages and uniformly distributed due to the unconditional blinding.

- **Distribution Equivalence:**
  - For \((m_0, z_0)\), in both cases, \( z_0 \) is the result of a valid run of the protocol, so the pair is identically distributed.
  - For \((m_1, z_1)\), the meta-reduction executes an honest run of the protocol, while the magic adversary uses a local computation method. This does not affect the distribution of the result, as it is the same as if the protocol had run with input \( t, m_1 \) and the reduction as the server.

Thus, we have:
\[
\Pr[V(x, y') = 1 | y' \leftarrow R_A^\Sigma(x)] = \Pr[V(x, y') = 1 | y' \leftarrow R_M(x)]
\]

### Conclusion

Since our efficient meta-reduction \( M \) can solve \( P \) by running \( R \) locally, the existence of such a reduction \( R \) contradicts the hardness of \( P \).
