### Notation Details

In this section, we extend the description of our notation introduced in Section 5.1. First, we elaborate on our implemented rounding function in Section A.1. Second, we present a basic example of a similarity metric used as part of our evaluations in Section A.2.

#### A.1 Use Case-Specific Rounding

As described in Section 5.1, we apply a use case-specific rounding function \( r(x_i) = x'_i \) to the input parameters \( x_i \) before computing the similarity metrics. This rounding function is designed to ensure that the parameters are within a practical range and to reduce the impact of minor variations. The specific rounding function used depends on the context and the nature of the data. For example, in the context of injection molding, the rounding function might be defined as follows:

\[ r(x_i) = \begin{cases} 
\lfloor x_i \rfloor & \text{if } x_i - \lfloor x_i \rfloor < 0.5 \\
\lceil x_i \rceil & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases} \]

This ensures that the parameters are rounded to the nearest integer, which is often a practical requirement in manufacturing processes.

#### A.2 Example of a Similarity Metric

To illustrate the use of similarity metrics, consider the Euclidean distance as a simple example. Given two vectors \( \mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n] \) and \( \mathbf{y} = [y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n] \), the Euclidean distance \( d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \) is defined as:

\[ d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - y_i)^2} \]

This metric can be used to measure the similarity between two sets of process parameters. A smaller Euclidean distance indicates higher similarity, while a larger distance indicates lower similarity. This metric is particularly useful in scenarios where the parameters are continuous and the goal is to find the closest match.
