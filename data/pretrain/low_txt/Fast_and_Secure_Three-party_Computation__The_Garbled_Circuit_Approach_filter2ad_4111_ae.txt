## Appendix A: Security Proof for Naor Commitment Variant

In Section 2.3, we defined a multi-bit variant of Naor's commitment in the CRS model. For \( n \)-bit strings, we need a CRS \( \in \{0, 1\}^{4n} \). Let \( G: \{0, 1\}^n \rightarrow \{0, 1\}^{4n} \) be a pseudorandom generator, and let \( \text{pad}: \{0, 1\}^n \rightarrow \{0, 1\}^{4n} \) be the function that prepends \( 3n \) zeroes to the front of its argument. The commitment scheme is as follows:

- **Com\(_{\text{crs}}(x; r)\)**: Set \( C = G(r) + \text{crs} \cdot \text{pad}(x) \) with arithmetic in \( \text{GF}(2^{4n}) \); set \( \sigma = (r, x) \).
- **Chk\(_{\text{crs}}(C, \sigma = (r, x))\)**: Return \( x \) if \( C = G(r) + \text{crs} \cdot \text{pad}(x) \); otherwise return \( \bot \).

**Theorem 2.** This commitment scheme satisfies the properties of binding and hiding given in Section 2.3.

**Proof.**

- **Hiding**: Hiding follows easily from the security of the PRG \( G \). Specifically, for all CRS and \( x \), a commitment is generated as \( C = G(r) + \text{crs} \cdot \text{pad}(x) \). Since \( r \) is chosen uniformly, the result is pseudorandom.
  
- **Binding**: An adversary succeeds in equivocating if and only if it can produce \( C, r, x, r', x' \) with \( x \neq x' \) and:
  \[
  C = G(r) + \text{crs} \cdot \text{pad}(x) = G(r') + \text{crs} \cdot \text{pad}(x')
  \]
  \[
  \Rightarrow \text{crs} = \frac{G(r) - G(r')}{\text{pad}(x') - \text{pad}(x)}
  \]
  Since \( x \neq x' \) and \( \text{pad} \) is injective, \( \text{pad}(x') - \text{pad}(x) \neq 0 \) and indeed has a multiplicative inverse in the field.

  Let us call a CRS "bad" if there exist \( r, r', x \neq x' \) satisfying the above property. Clearly, an adversary's success probability in equivocation is bounded by \( \Pr[\text{CRS is bad}] \).

  There are at most \( 2^{2n} \) values of \( G(r) - G(r') \) and at most \( 2^n \) values of \( \text{pad}(x') - \text{pad}(x) \) (under the standard encoding of \( \text{GF}(2^k) \) into \( \{0, 1\}^k \), we have \( \text{pad}(x') - \text{pad}(x) = \text{pad}(x' \oplus x) \)). Hence, there are at most \( 2^{3n} \) bad values of CRS. It follows that the probability of a random CRS \( \in \{0, 1\}^{4n} \) being bad is at most \( \frac{1}{2^n} \).
