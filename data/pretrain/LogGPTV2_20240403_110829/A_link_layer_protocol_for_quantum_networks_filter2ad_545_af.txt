### Appendix

#### A. Quantum Prelude

This section provides a brief introduction to quantum information, including concepts such as entanglement, fidelity, and quantum bit error rate (QBER). It also explains how these concepts relate to memory lifetimes.

##### A.1 Qubits and States

A quantum bit (qubit) is a two-level system, where the two levels are typically denoted \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \) (ket notation) and called the basis states of the qubit. These levels can be, for example, two energy levels of an electron spin or, when considering transmitting qubits, vertical and horizontal polarization of a photon, presence or absence of a photon, or a time-bin of early and late. Unlike a classical bit, which is either \( |0\rangle \) or \( |1\rangle \), a qubit can exist in a superposition of these states. Mathematically, a state \( |\phi\rangle \) of a qubit is written as:

\[
|\phi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle
\]

where \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) are arbitrary complex numbers with the constraint that \( |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \). The basis states \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \) form a basis for \( \mathbb{C}^2 \). Some common states are:

\[
|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
\]

Measuring a qubit in the standard (Z) basis ( \( |0\rangle, |1\rangle \) ) gives measurement outcomes '0' (i.e., \( |0\rangle \)) or '1' (i.e., \( |1\rangle \)). Measuring a qubit in the state \( |\phi\rangle \) as in the equation above in the standard basis yields the outcomes 0 or 1 with the following probabilities:

\[
P[\text{"measuring 0"} | \text{Z-basis}] = |\alpha|^2, \quad P[\text{"measuring 1"} | \text{Z-basis}] = |\beta|^2
\]

Measuring a qubit in the X- or Y-basis yields outcomes with probabilities:

\[
P[\text{"measuring 0"} | \text{X/Y-basis}] = | \langle X/Y, 0 | \psi \rangle |^2, \quad P[\text{"measuring 1"} | \text{X/Y-basis}] = | \langle X/Y, 1 | \psi \rangle |^2
\]

where \( \langle \cdot | \cdot \rangle \) is the inner product.

##### A.2 Entangled States

If qubit A is in a state \( |\phi_1\rangle \) and qubit B is in the state \( |\phi_2\rangle \), then their joint state (at possibly remote nodes) is given by the tensor product of the individual states \( |\phi_1\rangle_A \) and \( |\phi_2\rangle_B \):

\[
|\text{"joint state"}\rangle = |\phi_1\rangle_A \otimes |\phi_2\rangle_B
\]

Entangled states are those that cannot be expressed as a tensor product of individual states. For example, the Bell states are maximally entangled states:

\[
|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle + |11\rangle)
\]
\[
|\Phi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle - |11\rangle)
\]
\[
|\Psi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|01\rangle + |10\rangle)
\]
\[
|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|01\rangle - |10\rangle)
\]

These states have the property that measuring one qubit instantaneously determines the state of the other, regardless of the distance separating them.

#### B. Estimating Fidelity

Fidelity is a measure of the similarity between two quantum states. To estimate the fidelity of a quantum state, test rounds can be interspersed in the protocol. These test rounds involve preparing and measuring known states, and comparing the measured outcomes with the expected ones. The fidelity \( F \) can be estimated as:

\[
F = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left| \langle \psi_i | \rho_i | \psi_i \rangle \right|
\]

where \( \rho_i \) is the density matrix of the prepared state, and \( |\psi_i\rangle \) is the ideal state.
