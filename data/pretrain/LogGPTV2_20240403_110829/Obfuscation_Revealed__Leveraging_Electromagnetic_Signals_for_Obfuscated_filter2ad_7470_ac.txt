#### 9.2 Experimental Results on the Meaning of Test Traces

**Figure 7: Accuracy when computing the mean over t = [1, 2, 3, ..., 10] samples per binary in the test dataset.**

We observed an improvement in all scenarios when calculating the mean over t execution measurements from the same binary in the test dataset. This suggests that if the number of executions or measurements per unknown binary is not a limiting factor for the malware analyst, then computing the mean over t traces will result in a more accurate prediction. This process is typically used in the side-channel domain, as in [8].

Interestingly, we did not observe a straightforward improvement for MLP and CNN. One possible reason is that the random user environment changes with each execution, even though the binary remains unaltered. This causes the measurement trace to change, and when calculating the mean, the patterns of features that may help MLP and CNN to make correct predictions may be mixed or altered. Conversely, NB and SVM, which are simpler models, may not be able to model these patterns, and computing the mean can be seen as noise reduction instead.

- For type and novelty classification, the accuracy reaches 100%.
- There is a significant performance improvement for SVM and NB in scenarios where the initial accuracy was lower.
